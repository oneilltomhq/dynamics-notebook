dynamics notebook · rung 13 — least action

The whole path chooses itself.

Rung 1 chased the future one frame at a time: state makes force, force nudges state. Now zoom out. Freeze the two endpoints of a journey and ask: of all the ways to get from here to there, which one does nature take? Answer — the one that makes a single number, the action, sit at the bottom of its valley. And the path that wins obeys, node for node, the exact same a = F/m you already built.

the path q — position vs time action S = ∫(T − V) dt residual ∂S/∂q — the force that fixes the path

01 · let a wrong guess fall downhill

Pin both ends, release the middle

The amber curve is a candidate trajectory — height plotted against time, both ends nailed down. It starts as a dumb straight guess. Every frame each interior point slides a hair downhill on the action: q −= lr · ∂S/∂q. Watch it settle into the true motion. Switch the world it lives in — free flight, gravity, or a spring well — and the same descent finds line, arc, and sine.

/world/strength g55

Drag either endpoint dot to re-aim the journey — the interior re-relaxes live. In the spring well the extremal is a sine: you just re-derived rung 1's oscillator from the other end, as the laziest path instead of a marched-forward force. Free flight is the straight line — Newton's first law is the shortest action, not just the shortest distance.

02 · why that path and no other

The true path sits at the bottom of a valley

Take the settled path and shove one interior node up or down by an amount δ. Its action climbs — either direction costs more. Slide δ yourself and read the action curve on the right: a bowl, its floor exactly where the descent left the node. "Least action" is not poetry; it is literally the minimum of this curve, and the slope at the floor — dS/dδ = 0 — is what "the path is settled" means.

/nudge/δ (middle node)0

Push δ far and the bowl steepens — action punishes big detours quadratically, the same way a spring's energy punishes displacement. Snap δ back to 0 (double-tap the slider) and you are back on the trajectory nature picks.

03 · a race the true path always wins

Throw random detours at it — none get under

Here is the true path plus a fistful of smooth random impostors, same endpoints. Each bar is one path's action. The amber bar — the settled trajectory — is always the shortest. Roll new impostors forever; you cannot beat it, because you already slid to the bottom.

This is the whole trick of the Lagrangian picture: instead of a rule that steps forward, a competition over entire histories that the real one wins. Feynman's leap was noticing a quantum particle secretly tries all these paths — and the winner survives because its neighbors agree in phase.

04 · the punchline

"Settled" means m·a = F at every node

Set the residual ∂S/∂q to zero and turn the crank. What drops out is the discrete Euler–Lagrange equation — and it is Newton, hiding:

// slide each node downhill on the action: r = (2*q[i] - q[i-1] - q[i+1]) + (F(q[i])/m)*dt*dt; // ∂S/∂q, scaled q[i] -= lr * r; // r = 0 ⇔ (q[i+1] - 2q[i] + q[i-1]) / dt² = F(q[i]) / m // └────────── a (discrete) ──────────┘ └── F/m ──┘ // ⇔ m · a = F ← rung 1, exactly

The two curves below are the discrete acceleration a = (q₊ − 2q + q₋)/dt² and the force per mass F/m = −V′(q)/m, node by node. While the path relaxes they disagree — that gap is the leftover residual pushing it. When it settles, they lie on top of each other. Minimizing the action over the whole history and stepping a=F/m forward frame by frame are the same law read two ways.

"On-shell" is the physicist's word for a path that obeys the equations of motion — one where cyan and magenta coincide. Knock it off, and the residual (the vertical gap) is the machinery of least action doing its job in real time.
