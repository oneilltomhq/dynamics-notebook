01 · one ray, one number

One ray, one number

Send one ray through matter and track one number: transmittance T — the fraction of light still alive. Every small step costs the ray a fixed fraction of whatever it has left, in proportion to the density it's passing through. That rule has a name — Beer-Lambert — and a shape you already know. Drag on the canvas to paint more density in the beam's path.

T = 1 for each step ds along the ray: T *= exp(−σ · ρ(x) · ds) // dT/ds = −σρT — rung 1's decay ODE, s instead of t
/ray/extinction σ10.0

Look at the curve, not the beam: T never falls linearly — it falls exponentially, fast through the lumps, flat in the gaps, exactly like rung 1's damped spring bleeding energy. Transmittance is a state; the matter it crosses makes its force; the force remakes the state. Same atom, new axis — distance instead of time.

02 · the cloud gets a shadow

The cloud gets a shadow

Now run that one ray thousands of times. The cloud below is rung 3's fbm noise, baked onto a grid. For every cell, march a short ray toward the sun and total up the density it crosses — Beer-Lambert tells you how much sunlight survives the trip. Bright where the trip was cheap, dark where the cloud shades itself. Drag the sun around it.

occ = 0 for a few short steps toward the sun: occ += ρ(sample) · ds light = exp(−σ · occ) // section 01 again — the ray to the sun
/sun/angle°130
/cloud/extinction σ0.40

Nothing here knows what a shadow is. The sun-facing rim glows and the core goes flat and dark purely because the occlusion ray crosses more density on the way in — self-shadowing falls out of repeated Beer-Lambert, for free. Every movie cloud you have ever seen — every nimbus in every render farm — is this exact loop, run in 3D with more steps.

03 · which way light bounces

Which way light bounces

Extinction says how much light survives. It doesn't say where the survivors go. When light hits a droplet it scatters — and the direction it prefers is one function of one angle: the Henyey-Greenstein phase function, with a single knob g. Left: the lobe itself. Right: a cloud disc with a sun you can drag around it — the eye at the bottom is you.

HG(cosθ, g) = (1/4π) · (1 − g²) / (1 + g² − 2g·cosθ)^1.5 // g>0 forward · g<0 back · g=0 any way
/phase/anisotropy g+0.60

Drag the sun behind the disc — opposite the eye — and it flares. That's forward scattering: real cloud droplets have g ≈ 0.8, so a backlit cloud fires most of its light straight on toward you. The silver lining is not poetry; it's the sharp end of this lobe. Pull g negative and the flare swaps sides — glass beads and retroreflectors live there. At g = 0 the lobe is a circle: light forgets where it came from.

04 · the flash inside

The flash inside

Put all three together: the section-02 cloud, sun occlusion, and the HG lobe deciding how much of the scattered sun heads your way. Then add one more light — click inside the cloud to set off a flash. Each cell hears the flash through distance falloff and one Beer-Lambert tap taken halfway to it, so the light dies where the cloud is thick between you and the bolt.

The flash's occlusion is one honest tap — density sampled at the midpoint, applied over the whole path — a one-sample approximation of the full Beer-Lambert integral, and it's already enough to make the glow hug the thin parts of the cloud, exactly like intracloud lightning revealing structure from within. The flash itself decays on rung 1's exponential envelope after every click. One thing is still fake: you chose where the bolt goes. Rung 10 takes that away — a field will decide the path, and the shape it grows is the shape of lightning.
