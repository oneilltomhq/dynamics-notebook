dynamics notebook · rung 7 — sync

Many springs learn to agree.

Six rungs taught one thing to move. The last rung is about many. Give each thing its own beat, let it faintly feel the others, and a shared rhythm forms that nobody is conducting. This is the page where the word conductor stops being a metaphor.

phase / rhythm — the star velocity / flow push randomness field

01 · a spring on phase

Two metronomes

The whole state of a rhythm is one number: a phase θ that runs around a circle. Two metronomes, slightly different natural speeds ω. Uncoupled, they tick past each other — watch the tick lamps drift out of step, together, apart, together, the visual version of two close notes beating. Now let each one feel the other, weakly.

θme' = ωme + K · sin(θother − θme) // that sin term is the entire idea
/couple/K0.00

(Whisper: these are drawings, not physics — the arm angle is just sin of phase. Real metronomes have real pendulums; the sync math does not care.) The coupling term sin(θother − θme) is just a spring — a spring on phase. Ahead of you, it pulls me forward; behind, it drags me back. Rung 1's atom, aimed at timing instead of position. And like any spring against a steady pull, it has a limit: lock needs K ≥ Δω. Below that, the strip chart slips forever; above, it flattens to a constant truce.

02 · the crowd

A hundred fireflies

Kuramoto's model, the real thing: N oscillators, natural frequencies drawn from a gaussian (mean Ω, spread σω), everyone coupled to everyone — θi' = ωi + (K/N)·Σ sin(θj − θi). Each dot flashes when its phase crosses zero. The circle on the right shows every phase as a tick on a rim, and the amber arrow is the order parameter r — the length of the average of all the phases, 0 for incoherent twinkle, 1 for unison. Start below threshold. Then raise K and watch order snap in.

/swarm/K0.30
/swarm/spread σω0.40

There is a sharp threshold — below it, more coupling does almost nothing; above it, order snaps in. Another regime cliff (rung 6 taught you to spot these); the muted readout estimates where it sits for the current spread. r is the loudness of the crowd's single voice. Real fireflies on riverbanks in Thailand do this every night, thousands of them, with no firefly in charge.

03 · neighbors only

The wave option

Same oscillators, different wiring. A ring of 48, natural frequencies identical, and each one hears only its two neighbors. Global coupling asks "what is everyone doing?"; local coupling asks "what are you two doing?". That one change buys waves. Nudge a single oscillator and watch the disagreement propagate both ways around the ring — rung 1's string pulse, running on phase. Then try the twist.

/ring/K3.0

Global coupling gives unison; neighbor coupling gives waves — the pulse in a spring chain and the sweep of a swung beat are this. The twist is stranger: one full 2π of winding laid around the ring. Local rules can only smooth phase differences, never delete a whole lap — so it can never unwind to unison. A topological twist, trapped: a permanently traveling wave that global coupling would never permit.

04 · recompose

The conductor, decomposed

The finale, built from the ladder's own parts list. A chain of 12 nodes. Each node keeps a phase with neighbor coupling — rung 7. Each time its phase crosses zero it kicks a sideways spring — rung 1, a beat driving a body. A little OU jitter rides the velocity — rung 2, tiny σ. One knob, /chain/K, is a "conduct" control built from primitives: at 0 every joint keeps its own drifting time, loose and human, behind the beat; high, the chain locks into machine unison.

A conductor, decomposed into primitives you now own: phase springs (rung 7) + position springs (rung 1) + dice (rung 2). The ladder closes here, and it closes on the original goal: you can now watch a texture you love — a loose shuffled groove, a locked grid, grass in wind, fireflies — and say which rung each ingredient came from. That was the whole point. It is yours now.
