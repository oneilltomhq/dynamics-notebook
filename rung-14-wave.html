dynamics notebook · rung 14 — the wave field

Line up the springs. You built a field.

Rung 1's string was a chain of neighbor springs. Pack them dense and stop counting nodes: the displacement becomes a smooth function u(x,t) and the chain's law becomes the wave equation. Add one term — a spring pulling every point back toward zero — and the field gains mass: this is the Klein–Gordon field, the simplest thing in all of particle physics, and its dispersion is E² = p² + m² staring right back at you.

01 · the continuum limit, live

Pluck it — a wave is a lattice of springs agreeing

Grab the string and pull. Let go. The bump splits into two travelers that bounce off the pinned ends forever (until damping bleeds them). Each node is doing nothing but rung 1's neighbor-force — a = c²(u₊ + u₋ − 2u) — yet together they carry a shape across space at speed c. That second difference is the second derivative ∂²u/∂x²: the wave equation is hiding in the chain.

Turn the mass term on and pluck again: the crisp travelers smear and ripple in place — the field now resists just existing, not only bending. Massless (m² = 0) is light: any shape rides along rigidly at exactly c. Massive is matter: shapes disperse, and the field jitters even where it is flat.

02 · one curve rules them all

How fast a wave goes depends on its wavelength — unless it's light

Every pure sine mode has a wavenumber k and swings at a frequency ω(k) = √(c²k² + m²). Massless: a straight line — every wavelength travels at the same speed c, so shapes hold together (that is why light has no blur). Massive: a hyperbola that never dips below ω = m. Click the curve to launch that mode below.

/field/mass m0.40

Read the axes as physics: k is momentum p, ω is energy E. The curve is E² = p² + m² — Einstein's relation for a free particle. The floor at k=0 is E = m: rest energy. Slide the mass and watch the whole spectrum lift off the ground.

03 · light keeps its shape, matter smears

The same bump, sent down two fields

Top string is massless, bottom is massive — identical Gaussian packet launched down each. The massless packet glides rigidly: all its component wavelengths share one speed, so they stay in step. The massive packet spreads: long and short wavelengths travel at different speeds and drift apart. A wavepacket is a chord; dispersion is the chord going out of tune.

This is exactly why a free electron's wavefunction blurs over time while a photon's pulse does not. Mass is the field disagreeing with itself about how fast to move.

04 · what standing perfectly still costs

Rest energy is the frequency of doing nothing

Make the field perfectly flat — no spatial variation at all, k = 0. A massless field just sits there: ω(0) = 0, nothing happens. A massive field can't: it oscillates in place at ω = m, its rest energy ticking whether or not it goes anywhere. That immovable minimum frequency is the mass gap — and the seed of E = mc².

/field/mass m0.55

The left dial is a massless field at k=0 (frozen); the right is massive (ticking at ω = m). Turn mass to zero and the right one stops too. Everything with mass is, at bottom, a little clock that runs even at rest.
