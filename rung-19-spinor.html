dynamics notebook · rung 19 — spinors

Turn it all the way around. It's not back yet.

Every rung so far, a full 360° turn brought things home. Matter is stranger. An electron — a spinor — needs two full turns, 720°, to truly return; one turn leaves it flipped to its own negative. This isn't a quirk, it's the reason fermions exist, why two electrons can't share a state, why the periodic table has shape. The machine underneath is SU(2), the double cover of rotations: the internal clock of matter runs at exactly half the angle of the world.

01 · the clock that runs at half speed

Spin the object once; the spinor only gets halfway home

Turn the amber object through an angle α. The cyan spinor arrow, its internal state, turns only α/2 — the half-angle that runs through all of spin. At α = 360° the object looks identical to where it began, but the spinor points the opposite way: the state has been multiplied by −1. Keep going to α = 720° and only now does the spinor come home to +1.

/rotation/angle α0°

The twisting ribbon says the same thing topologically: one turn tangles it and no amount of sliding frees it; a second turn, surprisingly, lets it slip loose — the belt trick. A 360° twist is real and detectable; a 720° twist is nothing at all. Matter can tell the difference between one turn and none.

02 · two spinors for every rotation

SU(2) wraps twice around the world of rotations

A rotation is stored as a unit quaternion q = (cos α/2, sin α/2 · n). Because everything is the half-angle, when the object turns a full 360° the quaternion only sweeps 180° — and the two quaternions q and −q, antipodes, describe the identical orientation. Rotations have two spinor names each. That two-to-one wrap is the double cover, and the −1 is the electron reporting which of the two it took.

The little frame on the left is the object's orientation; the ring on the right is the quaternion circle. Two dots, q and −q, ride it a half-turn behind the object and always sit opposite. The world of orientations is SO(3); its double cover SU(2) is the true home of spin — the same group whose local version is the weak force of the Standard Model.

03 · you can measure the minus sign

Rotate one beam of a split electron and watch it darken

Split a spinor into two paths, rotate one of them by α with a magnetic field, then recombine. The brightness is cos²(α/4). At α = 360° — a full, innocent-looking turn — the two halves come back exactly out of phase and cancel to darkness. Only at 720° do they blaze back to full brightness. This is a real experiment (Rauch, neutrons, 1975); the −1 is not bookkeeping, it is on the detector.

/one arm/rotation α360°

Sweep α and read the fringe: bright at 0°, dark at 360°, bright again at 720° — a 720° period baked into the fabric of matter. Everything with half-integer spin obeys it, and it is exactly why they stack instead of piling into one state — the exclusion principle, and the solidity of everything you have ever touched.

A spinor is the square root of a vector, and the half-angle is why. Dirac found the operator whose square is the wave equation — i∂ψ = (α·p + βm)ψ — and out fell the electron's spin, its antiparticle, and this very sign. In Witten's paragraph this is the third piece: fermions as sections of a spinor bundle.
