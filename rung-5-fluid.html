dynamics notebook · rung 5 — real fluid

The field learns to carry itself.

Rung 4's swirl was painted on — the field pushed the particles, and the particles could never push back. One new verb fixes that: carry. The velocity field transports its own contents, including itself. That single idea, plus one piece of honest bookkeeping, is a real fluid.

01 · carrying, alone

Advection, isolated

Strip everything else away. This velocity field is frozen — one big vortex and a gentle side stream, prescribed by a formula, never changing. The only new physics running here is the carrying step: dye rides the flow. Drag on the canvas to paint dye and watch the field wind it into spirals.

// advection, the whole thing: dye[x] = dye_before[x − u(x)·dt] // look backward along the flow, copy what you find

Advection = the bucket brigade. The field is frozen; only the dye moves. One line of physics: value at x now = value at (x − u·dt) a moment ago — look backward along the flow. That backward look is the whole trick (semi-Lagrangian), and it cannot blow up: you only ever copy values that already exist.

02 · why pressure exists

Same push, two fates

Now let the velocity field carry itself — and watch it go wrong. Both panels run the same solver and receive your exact same drag, mirrored. Left skips one step: projection. Right runs it. Drag in either panel; both get the push.

Projection answers one question every step: "where would stuff pile up?" — then adds exactly the pressure push that cancels the pile-up. Push fluid at a wall of fluid and it has nowhere to go but around: that forced detour is where the swirls come from. Rung 4 got incompressibility free from a rotation; here it's earned every frame by a little equation solve. Same property, two prices.

03 · the real thing

Stir it

Full solver, bigger grid. Every frame: add your forces, advect the velocity through itself, project away the pile-ups, advect the dye. That four-step loop is Jos Stam's "Stable Fluids" (1999) — the whole famous algorithm, and you have now seen every part of it. Drag to stir.

Iterations is "how hard pressure argues" — at 4 the water goes spongy and leaky, at 40 it snaps crisp. And this is the texture rung 4 could only imitate: eddies shed, swirls collide and merge, your stir keeps living after your hand stops. The field remembers — because now the field is the state, and the loop is the same as rung 1's: state makes force; force remakes state; the state just got 18,000 numbers wide.

04 · a reason to move

Heat rises — buoyancy

Until now every push came from your hand. Real storms push themselves. Give the fluid one more passenger — temperature — and one more force line, and the loop feeds itself: hot fluid gets shoved up, the flow carries the heat higher, the heat keeps shoving. A heater wanders along the floor; drag anywhere to add heat yourself.

// two new lines in the loop: T = advect(T) // temperature rides the flow exactly like dye… v += β · T · dt // …but this passenger pushes back — up
/buoy/β lift3.0
/buoy/heat0.90
/buoy/cooling0.992

This is the engine of every thunderstorm: heat makes rise, rise carries heat, and the tower builds itself — a real cumulonimbus adds one reinforcement (condensing vapor releases heat, so the updraft feeds harder the taller it gets), but the loop is this loop. The cloud you're watching was never sculpted; it grew. Now slide β to zero and watch the storm die into drifting smoke — no lift, no tower. One force term is the whole difference between haze and weather.

05 · the whole ladder in one loop

Nothing here was new

Read the loop once more, slowly. Add forces — that's rung 1's push and rung 2's kick, splatted into a grid. Advect — this rung's one new verb. Project — rung 4's divergence-free property, no longer free, earned each frame. Repeat at 60 fps.

Fluids were never a new kind of physics. Same loop, bigger state.
