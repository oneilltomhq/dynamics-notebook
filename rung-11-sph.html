The particles become the field.

01 · a particle is a bump

A particle is a bump

Ten particles, each carrying one number — cold cyan to hot magenta. Rung 3 answered "what is the field here" by pinning dice to a lattice. SPH answers with no lattice at all: each particle spreads its number over a smooth bump of radius h, and the value anywhere is the weighted blend of every bump that reaches it. Drag the particles. The field follows.

W(r, h) = (1 − r²/h²)³ for r < h, else 0 // one particle's bump A(x) = Σⱼ Aⱼ · W(|x−xⱼ|, h) / Σⱼ W // the field IS the sum of bumps
/kernel/radius h130

Rung 3's field was a landscape of pinned dice; this one is a landscape of carried bumps — where the particles go, the field goes. Widen h and the bumps merge into one smooth blur; shrink it and the continuum shatters into islands. That tradeoff is the whole art of SPH: h too small and the field falls apart, h too big and every detail smears.

02 · density is self-measurement

Density is self-measurement

Now point the kernel at the particles themselves. Each particle sums the bumps of its neighbors — ρᵢ = Σⱼ m·W(rᵢⱼ, h) — and gets a number that means "how crowded am I." Two hundred fifty particles below, idling on rung 2's tiny OU kicks. Drag to herd them; the button flips your hand from attract to repel. The readout follows your cursor.

/kernel/radius h48

No grid measured this — the particles measured each other. Loners read dim, huddles read bright, and the number updates the instant the crowd moves. Hold onto ρ: it is exactly the quantity pressure will push against in the next demo.

03 · dam break

Dam break

The real thing. Four hundred particles start as a column behind an invisible dam, and gravity takes it from there. Every substep runs the same short story: find your neighbors, measure your crowding, turn crowding into pressure, push apart along the bumps' slopes, share a little velocity with the neighbors, fall. The walls are rung 1's springs, standing guard at the edges. Drag inside the box to shove the water around.

// every substep, for every particle: neighbors = hash.query(xᵢ, h) // spatial hash — find who is near ρᵢ = Σⱼ m · W(rᵢⱼ, h) // crowding, self-measured (demo 02) pᵢ = k · max(0, ρᵢ/ρ₀ − 1) // crowding → pressure. clamped: no tensile goo aᵢ = −Σⱼ m (pᵢ/ρᵢ² + pⱼ/ρⱼ²) ∇W + viscosity + g // spiky gradient: push apart, downhill v += a·dt ; x += v·dt // rung 1's symplectic euler

Read the loop again — neighbors make forces, forces remake velocities, velocities remake positions. Rung 1's mantra, unchanged; the state just moved onto the particles. And name the cost honestly: with no grid, nothing knows where anything is, so every substep begins by rebuilding a spatial hash just to find who is near whom. That search is the tax SPH pays, 240 times a second, for owning no grid.

04 · what the bumps can't do

What the bumps can't do

Look at the water's surface in the dam break — it fizzes. A particle at the top has neighbors below but none above, so its bump sum reads falsely low, pressure misfires, and the surface never quite calms down. Add the neighbor-search tax on every substep and you have SPH's two standing bills. Rung 5's grid paid neither: it always knew where its numbers were, and its cells never ran out of neighbors. It just couldn't move.

So here is the obvious heresy: keep the particles — they carry the state, they go where the water goes — but borrow rung 5's grid for one step per frame. Splat the bumps onto the grid, let the grid do the force arithmetic where neighbors are free, then hand the answer back to the particles and throw the grid away. That handshake is MPM.
