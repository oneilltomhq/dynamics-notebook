dynamics notebook · study 3 — the kick

The drum lets go.

One real thing again: the kick drum — the sound that anchors nearly all recorded music since the drum machine. It decomposes fast. Pitch is rung 1's frequency wearing a perceptual name; the kick's signature whoomp is a spring whose stiffness collapses while it rings; the real drumhead underneath is rung 14's springs stretched over a circle, which is exactly why drums keep time instead of carrying tunes; and the moment you put the hit in a room, study 1 takes over. Every section makes sound — this one especially wants speakers or headphones with some low end.

01 · pitch is frequency, heard

Pitch is frequency, heard

Study 1 heard rung 1's decay; this study starts by hearing its other number. Slide f and press play: what changes is not "a property of the note" — it is the same oscillator swinging faster, and pitch is simply your ear's name for the rate. The range that matters here is the bottom of it. A kick lives between roughly 40 and 100 Hz, where hearing is least sensitive and rooms are hardest to control — everything difficult about the kick follows from where it sits on this one slider.

s(t) = sin(2π·f·t) // rung 1's swing — the ear calls f "pitch"
/tone/frequency f55 Hz

Ride the slider to the bottom and notice the tone getting harder to place — below about 20 Hz it stops being a pitch at all and becomes separate events, a flutter, a rhythm. That boundary is where study 4 lives: rhythm and pitch are the same number, f, heard on opposite sides of 20 Hz. The kick drum is the instrument that works both sides of the line at once.

02 · the drop

The drop — a spring losing its stiffness

Strike a real drum and, for a few milliseconds, the beater has stretched the head tight: extra tension means extra stiffness means — rung 1 — a higher frequency. Then the head relaxes and the pitch falls home. The classic synthesized kick keeps only that skeleton: one sine whose frequency starts high and decays exponentially to a floor, inside an amplitude that decays exponentially too. Both knobs are rung 1's τ, one aimed at the stiffness, one at the energy. But there is a real lesson in the code: you cannot write sin(2πf(t)·t). Multiplying a falling f by the whole elapsed t silently rewinds cycles the oscillator already lived — frequency is a rate, so it must be accumulated, not multiplied. The mantra again, smallest possible: the state is the phase, the frequency is its force.

f(t) = lo + (hi − lo) · e^(−drop·t) // stiffness collapsing home φ += 2π·f(t)·dt // integrate — frequency is a rate s(t) = sin(φ) · e^(−decay·t) // rung 1's envelope on top
/pitch/start hi160 Hz
/pitch/floor lo45 Hz
/pitch/drop rate45 /s
/amp/decay18.0 /s

The cyan curve is f(t) — watch how much of the drop is spent in the first fifty milliseconds. That early sweep through 160→80 Hz is the "click" and "knock" of the kick; the floor is its body. Slow the drop and the kick turns into a toy laser gun; speed it up and it becomes a dry thud. Four numbers, all of them rung 1, and you have walked the whole space from 808 boom to techno punch. One honest caveat: a real head's tension only detunes it by a semitone or two as it settles — this two-octave plunge is an 808 design convention, not literal membrane physics. It still reads as a drum because the ear tracks the fall itself, not its cause.

03 · the membrane

The membrane — why drums keep time instead of tunes

Underneath the cartoon sits a real object: a skin stretched over a circle — rung 14's line of springs, sewn into a sheet, clamped at the rim. Run it honestly (rung 15's leapfrog, u is the skin's transverse displacement) and strike it. A string's modes sit at 1, 2, 3, 4 — whole multiples, a harmonic series, a note. A circular membrane's modes sit at 1, 1.59, 2.14, 2.30, 2.65… — irrational neighbors that never agree on a common period. The ear, which names pitch by finding repetition, finds none, so the sound refuses to be a note and reads instead as an event — a when, not a what. That inharmonicity is the physical reason drums lean toward time and strings toward melody — a strong pull, not an iron law. The best counterexample proves the mechanism: the tabla's head is loaded with a disc of black paste (the syahi) that drags its modes back toward whole-number ratios, and so it sings a definite pitch. Engineer the inharmonicity away and the drum carries a tune. Click the head to strike it where you like; rim shots excite the lopsided modes, center hits favor the symmetric ones.

u′ = 2u − uᵖ + λ²∇²u, u|rim = 0 // rung 14's springs, clamped in a circle string modes: f·(1, 2, 3, 4…) — a note · membrane: f·(1, 1.59, 2.14, 2.30…) — a when

The recording plays the simulation itself — the skin's displacement at the amber listener cell (a stand-in for the sound it radiates), at the lattice's own sample rate — so the darkness and shortness you hear are earned, not styled. The one liberty is a single damping rate for every mode; a real head quiets its highs faster, so it would sound even darker. Section 02 now reads as a portrait: the synth kick replaces this inharmonic chord with a single falling partial, keeping just the envelope and the tension-drop and throwing away everything that made the drum a drum. The ear forgives it for the same reason it forgave study 1's delay network — perception bills for the envelope and the arrival, not the spectrum's fine print.

04 · into the room

Into the room

A kick drum is never heard in free air; it is heard in a room, and study 1 built the room. Here they meet: section 02's kick, convolved with the stereo answer of study 1's eight-line delay tank. One slider decides how much room rides along. And a tension appears immediately, the one every mix engineer negotiates: the room answers every hit, and its answer is study 1's exponential — seconds long — while kicks arrive twice a second. Long tail on a low note means each answer is still speaking when the next question lands, and the bottom octave turns to mud. That is why the kick, of all instruments, is sent to reverb reluctantly, briefly, or not at all — not taste, arithmetic: RT60 against the gap between hits.

heard = kick + wet · (kick ∗ room) // study 1's IR, study 3's hit the negotiation: RT60 vs the gap between hits — overlap is mud

Kick, room, figure: three studies, one sound.
