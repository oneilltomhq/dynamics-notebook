dynamics notebook · rung 16 — curved space

Walk a vector in a circle. It comes back turned.

Here is curvature with no equations at all: take an arrow, slide it around a closed loop always keeping it as parallel to itself as you can, and return to where you started. On a flat floor it points exactly as it began. On a sphere it comes back rotated — and the angle it turned is precisely the curvature bottled up inside the loop. That single fact is the seed of general relativity, and the same shape as every gauge field in the notebook.

01 · the trip that doesn't come home straight

Around a spherical triangle, a right angle appears from nowhere

Follow the amber arrow. It starts at the pole, walks straight down to the equator, turns to ride the equator a quarter-way round, then climbs straight back to the pole. At every step it is carried as parallel as the surface permits — it never chooses to rotate. Yet it arrives pointing 90° off. Drag the globe to look around; flip to a flat sheet and the same trip returns the arrow untouched.

/triangle/longitude90°

The leftover twist (magenta) equals the triangle's angle excess: its three corners add to more than 180°, and the surplus is exactly the turn. Flat triangles always sum to 180° — zero surplus, zero twist. Curvature is nothing but "how much the angles overflow."

02 · the twist counts the curvature it wraps

Bigger loop, more enclosed curvature, more twist

Now the loop is a circle of latitude — a hoop around the globe. Carry the arrow once around it and measure the turn. Shrink the hoop toward a point and the twist vanishes; grow it toward the equator and it approaches a full half-turn. The rule is exact: twist = ∫∫ K dA, the total curvature of the cap the loop bounds (here, its solid angle).

/hoop/colatitude θ70°

This is the Gauss–Bonnet theorem you can feel with your hands: a loop's holonomy reads off the curvature it encloses, no matter how you wiggle the loop. Curvature is a local density; the twist is its integral.

03 · why parallel lines don't stay parallel

Two straight-ahead walkers, and whether they meet

Send two travelers off side by side, each marching perfectly straight (a geodesic — no turning). Their gap ξ obeys one tidy law, the Jacobi equation d²ξ/ds² = −K·ξ. Positive curvature (sphere) bends them together until they collide at the far pole; zero curvature (plane) holds the gap fixed forever; negative curvature (saddle) flings them apart exponentially. Gravity, in Einstein's telling, is this: tides are geodesic deviation, and matter is what sets K.

/space/curvature K+0.60

Same starting gap, same "walk straight" rule, three fates decided by one number. Read K as the local matter density and you have the beating heart of the Einstein equation: tell space how to curve, and curvature tells parallel worldlines whether to focus or fly apart.

The deep echo: parallel transport is a connection, and the twist-per-area is its curvature. Swap "arrow" for "internal phase" and you have exactly rung 15's vector potential A and its field strength — and rung 20's lattice plaquette. Gravity and the gauge forces are the same geometry wearing different fibers.
