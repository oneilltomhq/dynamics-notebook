dynamics notebook · rung 20 — lattice gauge

The phase lives on the links.

The last idea, and the one that closes the loop. Rung 18 gave every point a phase; rung 16 carried an arrow around a loop and read off curvature. Put them together: store a phase on every link between points — the instruction for how to compare neighbors — and the curvature is the phase you pick up going around the smallest loop, the plaquette. This is Wilson's lattice gauge theory, the honest definition of every force but gravity, and the exact same holonomy you already met on the sphere.

01 · curvature is a loop of link phases

Go around the little square; the leftover phase is the field

Each link carries a phase — a tiny rotation you apply when hopping from one site to its neighbor. Walk a test phase all the way around one square, multiplying the four link phases with their directions, and what you come back with is the flux through that square: Φ = θ₁ + θ₂ − θ₃ − θ₄. Nonzero flux is curvature — literally rung 16's twisted arrow, now a twisted phase. Drop a flux vortex and watch the plaquettes light up.

Click any square to walk the test phase around its rim — the corner arrow spins up link by link and arrives rotated by exactly the flux inside. In the continuum this loop-sum becomes ∮A·dl = ∫B·dA: rung 15's magnetic field is the fine-grained limit of this plaquette. Gauge field on the links, curvature on the faces — the whole of electromagnetism, discretized.

02 · the potential is a choice; the flux is real

Scramble every link — the physics doesn't flinch

At each site you may freely redefine the local phase reference by any amount χ — a gauge transformation. Every link touching that site shifts by the difference of its endpoints' χ. Hit the button and watch: all the link phases churn, yet every plaquette flux holds dead still. The link values are convention, like rung 15's vector potential A; the plaquette flux is the physics, like B. Only gauge-invariant loops are real.

This is the same freedom as rung 18's global phase, now made local — different at every point. Demanding that the laws survive that freedom is not a bookkeeping nicety: it is the principle that forces the existence of the gauge field in the first place. Symmetry doesn't describe the force; it requires it.

03 · where all three pieces meet

A charge feels the flux it never touches

Split a charged particle around a tube of flux — flux it passes entirely through empty space, where the field is zero. Recombine. The two paths enclose the flux between them, so they disagree in phase by exactly Φ, and the fringe pattern shifts. The particle responds to the loop, not the local field: the plaquette, again. This is the Aharonov–Bohm effect, and it is rung 15's double slit and rung 16's holonomy and rung 18's phase, all revealed to be one thing.

Turn Φ and the whole fringe slides — brightness at the center is cos²(Φ/2), dark at 180°, bright again at 360°. The potential is doing the steering, in a region where there is nothing else to steer with. Curvature on the links is the most real thing in the theory.

This is where the ladder tops out. Swap the U(1) phase on each link for an SU(2) or SU(3) matrix — rung 19's spin group — and, because matrices don't commute, the plaquette gains terms where the field feeds on itself: gluons that pull on gluons, the whole Standard Model. Witten's three observations — gravity (rungs 16–17), gauge fields (rungs 15, 20), and spinor matter (rungs 18–19) — now sit on one page. Geometric Unity's wager is that a single geometry over a larger space produces all three at once; that final rung is not yet built, by anyone.
