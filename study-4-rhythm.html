dynamics notebook · study 4 — rhythm

Time gets a grid.

The last study of the sound cycle, and the strangest fit: rhythm looks like it belongs to culture, not dynamics. But take it apart and the rungs are all there. A beat is an oscillator slowed until its cycles become countable events. A drum pattern is arithmetic on that grid — and the patterns every culture converged on are the ones Euclid's algorithm generates. Groove is a deterministic lean of the grid, not noise. And the reason music moves things — lights, mixes, crowds, your neck — is that each hit is an impulse into some rung-1 spring, and everything after the hit is physics. Sections make sound; this page likes to loop, so there are stop buttons.

01 · the clock is a phasor

The clock is a phasor

Study 3 ended on a threshold: slide an oscillator below 20 Hz and pitch dissolves into separate events. Rhythm lives entirely below that line. A tempo of 120 beats per minute is an oscillator at 2 Hz — same state, same wrap, just slow enough to count. So the master clock of all rhythm software is the humblest state machine in this notebook: a phase that climbs from 0 to 1 and wraps. The wrap is the beat. Everything else on this page — patterns, swing, sidechain, ensembles — is decoration hung on that sawtooth.

φ += (bpm/60)·dt · wrap at 1 — the wrap is the beat a tempo is a frequency: 120 bpm = 2 Hz // rhythm is sub-audio pitch
/clock/bpm120

Push the slider to 200 and imagine continuing: at 1,200 bpm the ticks fuse into a 20 Hz drone, and you have crossed back into study 3's first section from the far side. Pitch and tempo are one dial with a perceptual seam in the middle — which is why a drum roll can almost have a pitch, and why sped-up tape turns grooves into growls.

02 · euclid's drum

Euclid's drum

Chop the beat into a grid of steps and a pattern is just a subset: which steps hit. Out of the thousands of subsets, the ones musicians actually play are strangely few — and almost all of them answer one question: how do you spread K hits over N steps as evenly as possible when N doesn't divide by K? That is a rounding problem — the same one a line-drawing algorithm solves pixel by pixel — and its solutions are computed by the remainder cascade of Euclid's gcd algorithm. Three over eight gives the tresillo that underlies son, reggaetón, and half of pop radio; five over eight the cinquillo; seven over twelve the bembé bell of West Africa. Number theory, audible.

hit(i) = ((i·K) mod N) < K // K hits, maximally even over N steps tresillo = euclid(3,8) · cinquillo = euclid(5,8) · bembé = euclid(7,12)
/pattern/steps N8
/pattern/hits K3
/pattern/rotate0

Rotate deserves its own sentence: the tresillo rotated is still the tresillo to a mathematician, but not to a dancer — rotation decides which hit carries the downbeat, and the ear assigns the "one" with real force. A pattern is an equivalence class of evenness; a groove is that class pinned to a downbeat.

03 · swing

Swing — the grid leans, the dice jitter

No drummer plays the grid. The deviations come in two species, and the notebook has a rung for each. Swing is deterministic: every off-numbered step arrives late by the same fraction of a step, every bar, forever — a phase offset applied by parity. That systematic lean is most of what "feel" means; jazz pushed it past a half-step, house music runs nearer ten percent. Humanize is rung 2: a small random jitter on every hit, dice instead of lean. Push both sliders and listen to how differently they break the machine — swing makes the loop walk; jitter makes it drunk. One is structure, the other is noise, and your ear tells them apart in a single bar.

tᵢ = gridᵢ + (i odd)·swing·step/2 + jitter·dice // lean (structure) + rung 2 (noise)
/feel/swing0.00
/feel/humanize0.000

The strip shows where the sixteen ticks actually landed against the grid lines. Swing moves the same ticks the same way every bar — the pattern is still periodic, just with a longer unit cell, which is why swing survives quantization arguments: it is quantized, to a bent grid. Jitter never repeats, and past a few percent the ensemble illusion collapses — the ear grants "human" only a narrow band of dice before it hears "sloppy."

04 · the hit kicks a spring

The hit kicks a spring

Here is why rhythm belongs in a dynamics notebook at all. A beat, by itself, is a schedule of impulses — and an impulse is rung 1's other input. Pull is a force held over time; a hit is velocity delivered all at once. So couple the pattern to a spring: every hit adds a kick of velocity, and between hits the spring does what springs do — overshoot, ring, settle. The dot below never teleports; it is played. Producers built an entire aesthetic out of the audible version: sidechain pumping, where the kick's beat-phase becomes an exponential envelope that ducks everything else in the mix, so the whole track breathes at the tempo. Press play: the pad you hear is being ducked by the kick's clock, and the amount is the depth slider.

on hit: v += impulse // the beat is a force on a schedule between: v += (−k·x − c·v)·dt · x += v·dt // rung 1 does the rest duck(φ) = 1 − depth + depth·e^(−6φ) // sidechain: the beat as envelope
/duck/depth0.60
/spring/damping ζ0.25

Watch the amber dot on its spring while the loop runs: it is doing exactly what your head does. Entrainment to a beat is not a metaphor — motor neurons phase-lock to auditory rhythm within a few cycles, which is rung 7 wearing a body. Every VJ rig, light show, and dancing crowd is this section: a pattern of impulses, a field of springs, and the physics between the hits doing all the expressive work.

05 · when clocks meet

When clocks meet

One clock is a solo. Two clocks are a band, and bands have a physics problem: no two tempos are ever exactly equal. Uncoupled, two metronomes a few bpm apart drift through every alignment — section 03 of study 2, heard as ticks instead of drawn as figures. Add coupling — each clock nudged toward the other's phase, the way musicians lean toward what they hear — and rung 7's result takes over: past a threshold that depends on the detune, the clocks capture each other and lock, usually to a compromise tempo neither was playing. Render the experiment below and listen in stereo: left ear, left clock; right ear, right clock. With coupling low they lace past each other; raise it and after a bar or two the flams tighten into a single ensemble tick.

dφ₁ = ω₁ + K·sin(φ₂−φ₁) · dφ₂ = ω₂ + K·sin(φ₁−φ₂) // rung 7, n = 2 locks when K > |ω₁−ω₂|/2 // coupling must out-pull the detune
/clocks/detune6.0 bpm
/clocks/coupling K0.00

The magenta trace is the phase difference between the clocks. Uncoupled it is a ramp — study 2's precession, one more time. Coupled past the threshold it bends and flattens: locked, with a constant lag (the slower clock forever slightly behind, exactly like rung 7's metronomes). This is the last rung this study needs, and it closes the sound cycle where it started: a room full of springs, all listening to each other, agreeing on when.
