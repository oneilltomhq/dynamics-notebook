dynamics notebook · study 1 — reverb

The room answers.

The rungs climb from one idea to the next; a study climbs back down. Take one real thing — the sound a room makes after the sound stops — and find the rungs inside it. Reverb turns out to be the ladder in miniature: it is a chord of damped springs, a swarm of rays, a wave in a box, and a delay network, all at once, and each of those is a page you have already read. Every section below makes sound — turn your volume to something kind.

01 · one spring, heard

One spring, heard

Rung 1 drew the damped spring; this page plays it. A single mode of a room — one way the air in it can stand and ring — is exactly rung 1's oscillator: a frequency, a decay time, nothing else. Ping it. That half-second of tone is the entire vocabulary of this study. Everything a room does to sound, it does by owning thousands of these, each with its own f and its own τ.

h(t) = e^(−t/τ) · sin(2πf t) // rung 1's answer, as audio // a room's impulse response is nothing but a sum of these
/mode/frequency f220 Hz
/mode/decay τ0.50 s

The amber curve is e^(−t/τ) — the same envelope that ended rung 1, the return stroke of rung 10, and every flash you have ever seen. Reverb is the audible name for this curve. When a hall is said to have "two seconds of reverb," it means the chord of springs takes two seconds to fall 60 dB down that exponential.

02 · the room is a chord

The room is a chord of springs

Which frequencies does a box own? The ones whose half-waves fit between the walls — rung 14's standing waves, in three directions at once. Each triple (l, m, n) of half-wave counts is one mode, one spring in the chord. Ping the room and every spring rings at once: rung 7's superposition, heard instead of seen. But count the comb below — a few hundred springs only reaches a low ceiling, and a pure mode sum sounds like a struck pipe, not a room. The fix is rung 2: above the ceiling the modes crowd so densely they stop being individuals and become statistics — decaying noise. Toggle the tail and the pipe becomes a hall.

f(l,m,n) = (c/2) · √( (l/Lx)² + (m/Ly)² + (n/Lz)² ) // rung 14, three ways at once IR(t) = Σ aᵢ e^(−t/τᵢ) sin(2πfᵢt) + noise(t) · e^(−t/τ) // springs + rung 2's dice
/room/width7.0 m
/room/depth5.0 m
/room/height3.0 m
/decay/RT601.60 s

The comb is the room's private scale — every line one mode, brightness its strength. Grow the room and the comb slides down and thickens: mode count grows like f³, so the 400 springs drawn here run out at a lower and lower ceiling, and the label tells you where. Below that ceiling a room is a musical instrument with resonances you could name; above it, a statistical process. That crossover — modes into noise, rung 1 into rung 2 — is the deepest single fact about reverb.

03 · the hall of mirrors

Early reflections — the hall of mirrors

Same room, opposite decomposition. Forget modes; follow rays, the way rung 9 followed light. A reflection off a wall sounds exactly like a copy of the source shining from a mirror room on the far side — so tile the plane with mirror rooms and the whole reflection pattern becomes a lattice of ghost sources, each firing the same click once. Every bounce keeps a fraction R of the energy — rung 9's Beer-Lambert, per wall instead of per meter — and every ghost arrives at its own moment, quieter with distance. Sum them and listen: this is rung 10's thunder with walls, the delay-sum made architectural. Drag the source and the listener; the pattern is yours to move.

for each mirror room (mx, my): t = d/343 · amp = R^bounces / d early = Σ clicks(t, amp) // rung 10's delay-sum, indoors
/walls/keep R per bounce0.75
/mirrors/max bounces4

The echogram underneath is the room's fingerprint: the direct click, a gap, then the first mirror arrivals, then a thickening crowd. Those first ten milliseconds are what your ears use to size a room before you have consciously heard it. Notice what this picture cannot do — a ray goes straight or it bounces; it never bends around a corner. Section 04 fixes that by refusing to approximate.

04 · the wave itself

No approximations — the wave itself

Rungs 02 and 03 were both shadows of the same object: the wave equation in a box. So run it. This is rung 15's FDTD lattice with pressure in place of the electric field — the identical leapfrog, the identical stencil. Click anywhere to drop a pulse and watch it do everything the previous sections only summarized: bounce off walls (the mirrors of 03), fold into standing patterns (the modes of 02), and — press the pillar button — bend around an obstacle, which no ray ever could. Then record: inject one impulse at the magenta source and write down the pressure at the amber listener, step by step. The list of numbers you get is the room's impulse response, earned rather than modeled.

u′ = 2u − uᵖ + λ²∇²u // rung 14's spring line, rung 15's lattice — λ² = ½ record u[listener] every step → the impulse response, measured not modeled
/walls/keep per step0.960

This is the ground truth the whole study rests on — serious hall design runs exactly this simulation in 3D. It is also why reverb belongs to this notebook at all: a room is nothing but rung 14's line of springs, closed into a box and listened to. The recording plays at the lattice's own sample rate, so what you hear is the simulation itself, not a rendering of it. Two dimensions gives the sound a hollow drag — in 2D a wavefront leaves a wake — but the bones are honest: direct, reflections, thickening tail, exponential surrender.

05 · the machine

The machine — a room made of delay lines

Simulating the wave is expensive; a hall's answer must sometimes come in real time, on a pedalboard. So build the room's behavior without the room. Eight delay lines stand in for eight wall-to-wall flight paths. Their outputs pour back into their inputs through a mixing matrix that scrambles energy the way rung 6's chaos folds trajectories — every echo of an echo lands in every line, so the tap count doubles and doubles until discrete echoes fuse into texture. The matrix is orthogonal, so it loses nothing; all the dying is done by two knobs from rung 1 — a gain g below one (friction) and a lowpass in the loop (air, felt, curtains — friction that prefers treble). This is the feedback delay network, and some flavor of it lives inside nearly every reverb pedal and plugin ever sold.

each sample: outᵢ = lowpass(delayᵢ) · gᵢ // rung 1: friction, twice delayᵢ ← input + Σⱼ Hᵢⱼ · outⱼ // H orthogonal — rung 6 does the mixing

/tank/RT602.00 s
/tank/damping0.30

Top strip: the full decay. Bottom strip: the first 120 milliseconds, magnified — watch the taps arrive singly, then double, then fuse. That fusing is the whole trick, and it is a mixing phenomenon: replace the matrix with the identity and the lines never talk, so you get eight lonely echoes forever instead of a room (chaos, or the lack of it, is audible). Notice the study has left physics — no wave, no wall, no meter of air anywhere in the code — and the ear forgives everything. The machine keeps only what perception bills for: arrival density and the exponential.

06 · where the ladder ends

Where the ladder ends

Every section so far leaned on one silent promise: the room is linear and time-invariant. Ping it twice, get the same answer twice; that is why a single impulse response could stand for the room, and why the whole thing decomposed into rung 1's springs at all. The demo below tests the promise. It renders the section-05 tank twice and overlays the two answers — cyan under magenta — then plots the difference. Now press the modulation button, the trick inside the lush studio reverbs of the 1980s: the delay lines breathe, lengths swaying a few samples. Render twice again. The answers no longer agree — the difference trace lights up — and with that, the impulse response stops existing. Not "gets harder to compute." Stops being a thing.

This boundary is exact. Everything nature builds from quiet air — every hall, cave, canyon, cathedral — sits inside it, and therefore inside this notebook: LTI reverb is rungs 1, 9, 10, 14 wearing different clothes. What lives outside is precisely what engineers made by breaking the promise on purpose: modulated tanks like this one (time-varying), shimmer reverbs that pitch-shift inside the loop (frequency is not conserved), gated and saturated reverbs (nonlinear), the freeze pedal (time-varying again). One more escapee is physical: the studio plate. A stiff sheet obeys a ∇⁴ bending law, so its wave speed grows with frequency and every echo arrives as a chirp — rung 14's Klein-Gordon dispersion, but with the opposite curvature and no place on this ladder yet. Where the promise holds, decompose; where it breaks, you can still simulate — state makes force, force remakes state — you just can no longer summarize.
