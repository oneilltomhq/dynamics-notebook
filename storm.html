01 · the sky

The sky

One scene, assembled from the whole ladder. The cloud is a density field baked once at load — metaball towers sheared into an anvil, eroded by fbm. Every frame it is relit with Beer–Lambert absorption: moonlight occluded through the density, plus up to three flash lights burning inside it. Bolts are midpoint displacement; thunder is a delay-sum of N-waves. Click the sky to call a strike down where you clicked.

Watch a full strike: a stepped leader crawls down dim for ninety milliseconds, the channel goes dark, then the return stroke fires — full channel, a flash light at the channel origin lighting the cloud from inside, ground glow at the strike point, the whole sky lifting, all decaying as exp(−22t). One to three restrikes follow down the same channel with less energy each, dark gaps between, like the real thing. Turn thunder on and slide the viewer close: the crack lands almost with the flash. Slide far: flash… count… rumble, stretched and low, because every point on the channel is a separate source at a separate distance. The bolt shape itself is a confessed cheat — rung 10 grows the physical leader step by step; this is its silhouette, drawn by rung 3's halve-the-amplitude trick on a curve instead of a field.

03 · the standing challenge

Rebuild it from memory

What you just watched is nimbus flattened to two dimensions. The production version is the same recipe, raymarched: a procedural cumulonimbus baked into a 3D density texture, a TSL raymarcher walking rays through it with Beer–Lambert absorption and a Henyey–Greenstein phase function for the silver lining, real point lights parked inside the volume for the flashes, bolt ribbons displaced on the CPU, and a storm director — a plain state machine — deciding when the sky breathes, flickers, or strikes. Same equations, same envelopes, same dice. The GPU only changes how many samples you can afford.

So the standing challenge: rebuild either version from memory. Not from this file — from the ladder. Start with a spring, add dice, let the dice live in space, light the density, displace the curve, sum the delays. Before every run, write down what you expect to see: how the anvil shears, when the thunder arrives, what exp(−22t) looks like against a dark sky. You will be wrong in places, and those wrong predictions are the curriculum — each one marks the exact rung you should climb again.
