dynamics notebook · study 2 — lissajous

Two springs, crossed.

Study 1 listened to one spring; this study watches two. Put one rung-1 oscillator on the x axis and another on the y axis and the point they steer draws a figure — the Lissajous curve, the shape a laser show is made of and the oldest picture of what two frequencies are doing to each other. The figure turns out to be arithmetic made visible: it closes when the frequencies are a fraction, precesses when they are almost one, and — pointed at a real stereo signal — becomes an instrument that lets you see sound. Some sections make sound; volume to something kind.

01 · one spring per axis

The figure two springs draw

No new physics — that is the point. Take rung 1's undamped spring twice: one copy swings the beam left-right, the other up-down, and neither knows the other exists. The drawing is pure composition. Start at 1:1 and sweep the phase: the same two springs give a diagonal line, an ellipse, a circle, the other diagonal — circular motion was never a new kind of motion, only two crossed springs a quarter-turn apart. The bright dot is the beam now; the trail is where it has been.

x(t) = sin(2π·fx·t + φ) // rung 1, aimed along x y(t) = sin(2π·fy·t) // rung 1 again, aimed along y — they never meet
/x/frequency fx0.60 Hz
/y/frequency fy0.60 Hz
/x/phase φ0.00

The trail is not evenly bright, and that is physics too: a laser projector steers its beam with galvanometer mirrors — rung 1 springs themselves — and the beam dwells wherever it moves slowest, so the turnarounds bloom. The brightness you see is time-spent-per-pixel, 1/|velocity|. Every oscilloscope, laser show, and the figures below inherit this glow for free.

02 · the ratio decides

Closed figures are fractions

Let the frequencies differ and the figure knots — but only certain figures ever close. The curve returns to its start exactly when both springs complete whole numbers of swings in the same interval: fx/fy = p/q, a fraction. And the figure is legible: it touches the top edge q times and the side edge p times, so you can read the fraction off the drawing — away from the degenerate phases where the curve folds onto itself and the tangencies halve (1:1 flat to a line, the bow to an arc). That is not a party trick — it is how frequency was measured. In 1857 Jules Lissajous bounced light between mirrors glued to two tuning forks and read the ratio from the figure, a century before electronics could count. Press play on each figure: the same two frequencies, sent to your ears instead of your eyes, are a musical interval. The octave, the fifth, the fourth — consonance is what a simple fraction sounds like; a closed figure is what it looks like.

closed ⇔ fx/fy = p/q // whole swings in a shared period read it: q lobes along the top · p along the side // the figure is the fraction
/x/phase φ1.57

The ear and the eye are running the same test. Two tones a simple fraction apart repeat their combined waveform quickly — the ear hears the repetition as smoothness; the beam closes its figure quickly — the eye sees a shape it can hold. Make the fraction ugly (7:9, 12:17) and both senses give up at the same moment: the sound roughens, the figure becomes a basket. That roughness is beating between the tones' harmonics crowding one spot on the ear — which is why these buttons play reedy tones, not bare sines: two pure sines a bad ratio apart sit too far apart to rasp. Period-counting is a large part of consonance, not the whole of it — harmonicity and a listener's ear-training both leave their mark, and how much is still argued over.

03 · detune

Almost a fraction — the figure breathes

Now break the fraction by a hair: fy = 2·fx + ε. The figure still tries to be the 1:2 bow, but the extra ε is phase that never stops accumulating — sin(2π(f+ε)t) is just sin(2πft + 2πεt), rung 7's drifting phase in disguise — so the bow rolls through every phase version of itself, once every 1/ε seconds. It never closes and it never repeats, yet it never surprises either: the point walks forever on the surface of a donut (one angle per spring) without ever finding home. This is quasi-periodic motion — the tame sibling of rung 6's chaos. Chaos mixes and forgets; detune only circles. And you have already heard it: two tones ε apart beat at exactly ε — the throb a guitarist tunes away is this precession, played into one ear.

sin(2π(f+ε)t) = sin(2πft + 2πεt) // detune is phase that never stops precession period = beat period = 1/ε // the eye and the ear agree on the number
/y/detune ε (figure)0.030 Hz
/tones/detune (audio)2.0 Hz

Watch the figure while the tones play: the audio detune slider and the figure's ε are the same knob at different scales. When a piano tuner slows the beating to nothing, they are steering this drawing toward closure by ear. Rung 7 begins exactly here — two metronomes ε apart — and adds the one thing this page withholds: a spring pulling the phases together. Detune is what the world does on its own; sync is what it does when you let the oscillators feel each other.

04 · the scope hears

Point it at a real signal

So far the springs were ours. Now hand the axes to a recording: left channel steers x, right channel steers y — the XY oscilloscope, the goniometer still glowing in every mastering room. The display is a correlation meter you can read like a face. A mono signal, identical in both ears, can only ever draw the 45° diagonal — one shared value, one line. Flip one channel's sign and it snaps to the other diagonal. Give each ear its own tone and the true Lissajous figure of section 02 appears, live. Then the payoff: ping study 1's delay-line room in stereo. The dry click stabs down the mono diagonal — then the reverb tail, decorrelated by two different mixes of the same eight delay lines, blooms off the line into area, and collapses back as it decays. Width, on this display, is not a metaphor. It is the geometry of L against R.

x = L(t) · y = R(t) // no model — the signal steers the beam mono → the diagonal · decorrelated → area // stereo width, measured not felt

The last button is study 1's feedback delay network with two output taps: the left ear sums the eight lines one way, the right ear another (same energy, different signs), so the tails agree less and less as the echoes of echoes pile up. Your ears do this measurement constantly — inter-aural decorrelation is a large part of why a hall feels around you while headphone mono sits inside your skull. The laser was never the instrument. The correlation was.

05 · the third spring

The third spring

Nothing stops at two. Add one more rung-1 oscillator on a z axis pointing out of the page and the flat figure becomes a curve in space — spin it slowly to see the depth. With three frequencies the closure rule survives untouched (all three pairwise fractions, and the curve is a knot; detune any one and it precesses on a higher-dimensional donut), and so does the moral of this study: at no point did the springs interact. Line, circle, bow, knot — every shape here is rung 1, copied, phased, and listened to jointly. Composition is the whole trick, and it is the same trick the rest of the notebook plays: nothing new was added to the atom; we only asked more of them at once.

x = sin(2πf₁t) · y = sin(2πf₂t) · z = sin(2πf₃t) // a third spring, nothing else all ratios rational → a closed knot · else → precession on a 3-torus
/z/frequency f₃1.50 Hz
/z/amount0.80

Set the z amount to zero and you are back at section 01 exactly — the 2D scope was always the 3D figure seen edge-on. This is where the study hands off to practice: a laser show's whole vocabulary — the spinning ellipse, the flower, the sphere-knot — is these three sliders plus time, and the projector doing the drawing is steering with the very springs it draws.
