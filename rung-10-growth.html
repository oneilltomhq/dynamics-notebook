01 · the invisible ladder

The invisible ladder

Before anything strikes, the sky does mathematics. A charged cloud base sits at potential φ = 1 along the top, the ground at φ = 0 along the bottom, and the air between must agree with both. The rule of agreement is the smoothest one possible: every point relaxes toward the average of its four neighbors — Laplace's equation, ∇²φ = 0. Watch the sweeps run; you have met this solver before.

repeat until calm: // Gauss–Seidel relaxation φ[i,j] = ¼ (φ[i−1,j] + φ[i+1,j] + φ[i,j−1] + φ[i,j+1]) // rung 5 solved exactly this to cancel pile-ups — same solver, new question

Nothing in this picture moves. No particle, no velocity — a field holding its breath. The glowing bands are equipotentials, contour lines of electrical height, and they space themselves out as the solve converges. The corner label counts sweeps and shows the residual — how loudly the field still argues with itself. Rung 5 buried this exact relaxation inside every fluid frame to find pressure; here it stands alone, because in the next section the field itself becomes the whole story.

02 · growth rolls dice on the field

Growth rolls dice on the field

Now give the field something to decide. A channel starts at the top center — one conducting site. The channel is held at the cloud's potential, so clamp it to φ = 0 and make the ground the attractive boundary at φ = 1. Relax as before. Then roll dice: every empty neighbor of the channel is a candidate, and its chance of joining is proportional to φη at that spot. Grow one site, re-relax, roll again. That is the whole dielectric breakdown model — Niemeyer, Pietronero and Wiesmann wrote it down in 1984, and lightning has looked computable ever since.

for c in empty neighbors of channel: w[c] = φ[c]^η grow one site, chosen with probability w[c] / Σw // dice, loaded by the field
/growth/η exponent2.0

The violet dots are the candidates — rung 2's dice, loaded by the field. η is the only knob, and it sweeps a whole zoo. At η = 0 the dice ignore the field entirely and a mossy blob crawls out (the Eden model). Near η = 1 every candidate is weighted by the local field and a dense fern grows — diffusion-limited aggregation. By η = 2 to 3 the strong sites win so often that the structure goes sparse and branched: lightning. One exponent, moss to fern to bolt. Set it, restart, and the same dice make a different creature.

03 · the return stroke

The return stroke

The downward crawl you watched in section 02 is the stepped leader, and in real skies it is dim — a scout current feeling for a path. The flash comes after contact. The moment the channel touches ground the circuit closes, and a return stroke re-illuminates the finished tree from the ground up, all at once. Because every site remembered its parent as it grew, tracing the ground hit back to the origin recovers the main channel — the spine burns brightest, the side branches only flicker.

/stroke/restrikes2

The flash envelope is e(t) = exp(−22t), and that should look familiar: rung 1 ends every dance the same way — a burst of energy, then exponential decay toward rest. Every flash you have ever seen ends in the same exp curve. Real strikes usually restrike, too: the channel stays ionized for a fraction of a second, so two or three strokes reuse it at decaying energy. The flicker in real lightning is the restrikes.

04 · thunder is geometry

Thunder is geometry you can hear

A bolt is not a point source — it is kilometers of line source, and every glowing segment makes its own bang at the same instant. Give the scene a scale: one cell ≈ 30 m, so this channel is roughly two kilometers of shocked air. Sound crawls at 343 m/s, so each segment's bang reaches you at its own moment, quieter with distance — and smeared wider with distance too, so the far tail arrives as rumble while the near part stays a crack. Sum them all and you have thunder. Drag the listener along the ground, then press listen.

for each glowing segment: t = d / 343 · amp ∝ bright / d · width ∝ d thunder = Σ N-waves(t, amp, width) // delay-sum: geometry, heard

This is rung 7's superposition wearing ear protection: many tiny sources, one sum, and all the structure lives in the arrival times. The crack at the front is the near part of the channel landing almost all at once; the rumble is the far tail, smeared out by distance. Move the listener and the same bolt sounds different — thunder is not a property of the bolt, it is a property of where you stand. One loose thread remains: the bolt grew on a grid, and the cloud was a field pinned to that grid. Rung 11 cuts the grid away and asks the stranger question — what if the particles themselves become the field.
