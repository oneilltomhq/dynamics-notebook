dynamics notebook · rung 8 — the gravity well

The spring pulls hardest far from home. Gravity pulls hardest close.

One change to the middle line — pull ∝ 1/d² instead of −k·d — and orbits stop being polite. Add a cross product and the whole sky spirals. This rung is the force model that runs three.js's attractors particles example, and (three wells of it) oneilltom.com. One color code throughout:

the well — home, and the pull toward it velocity — v the push — gravity + swirl

01 · the pull, turned inside out

Same launch, two laws

Two balls per panel, identical launches: parked at radius 1.2 and 2.0, kicked sideways at 0.8× circular speed. Left: rung 1's spring, pull = −k·d. Right: gravity, pull = G/d². Three tells to watch for. The spring's ellipse is centred on home; gravity's has the well at one focus — it swings close on one side. The spring ball moves gently everywhere; the gravity ball sprints through its close pass and crawls at the far end. And count laps: the two spring balls finish every orbit together no matter the size (isochrony — why rung 1's k set a frequency), while gravity's outer ball falls further behind every lap (Kepler's third law).

Relaunch a few times. Weak kicks make gravity's ellipse skinny — the close pass dives nearer the well and the speed difference gets violent. That dive is the subject of the next section.

02 · the landmine at d = 0

One bad frame invents energy

1/d² has a singularity: as d → 0 the pull → ∞. Real orbits never care — the fine print of the integrator does. Your loop samples the force once per step and believes it for the whole step. Drop a ball almost straight at the well: near the core it crosses the entire deep-gravity region in one step, reads one colossal force sample, and leaves with speed the physics never owned. Energy from nothing — the slingshot glitch. The fix is a lie called softening: d² += s². Inside a small core, pretend gravity stops growing; outside, truth as usual.

/well/softening s0.00

At s = 0, watch the top-speed counter and say goodbye. At s ≈ 0.3 the same drop swoops through, comes back, and traces a rosette — the orbit precesses, which is the price of the lie (softened gravity isn't exactly 1/d², so the ellipse won't close). Tuning s is a real design knob: tighter core → sharper, more Kepler-feeling swoops, but closer to the landmine.

03 · swirl from a cross product

Gravity digs the hole. The swirl keeps you out of it.

Gravity plus drag has one ending: everything spirals down the drain. The attractor trick adds one force — axis × toAttractor, scaled by the same g as gravity. It's tangential (a cross product is perpendicular to both its inputs), so it never pulls in or out; it only turns fall into orbit. And because it rides g, it's strongest exactly where gravity is strongest — its magnitude g·d peaks right at the softening core's edge, so the fastest circulation wraps the deepest gravity: spiral arms for free. Rung 4 bought swirl by rotating a noise slope; this rung buys it by rotating the pull itself.

for (const well of wells) { to = well.p − p // full vector — the swirl needs its length d² = |to|² + s² // softened: no landmine at the core g = G / d² // the pull, inverse-square v += to/√d² · g · dt // gravity — along the unit direction v += (axis × to) · g · SPIN · dt // swirl — tangential, magnitude ∝ g·d } v *= e−DRAG·dt // drag — eats what the swirl pumps in p += v · dt
/well/spin1.40
/well/drag2.00

Kill a term, get a different universe, again: drag 0 → the swirl pumps unopposed and the disc puffs into a cloud. Spin 0 → drag wins and the crowd drains into the core. Both on → a disc that circulates forever, because the swirl does work against the drag every frame. One subtlety earns the disc its width: every particle carries its own mass, so each settles at its own radius — identical particles would all collapse onto one thin ring. (The example calls this particleMassMultiplier; the site calls it b.pull.) Color is speed, slow → fast, exactly like the three.js example.

04 · the full machine

Three wells trade one sky

Everything so far, times three. Each well roams on slow incommensurate sines — rung 2's job, wearing a deterministic costume — and has a temperament: the heavy (deep gravity, lazy swirl), the spinner (shallow, violent, counter-rotating), the drifter (in between). Each also has a mood, a slow swing between dormant and raging, so the sky has real lulls and real surges. Bodies get traded: a lull at one well is exactly when a neighbour's surge steals its disc. The dot riding each ring shows that well's spin direction and tempo.

Click anywhere on the stage: a shockwave scatters the crowd, the wells gather it back — the same click that splashes oneilltom.com. This panel is that site's step() minus one dimension: same G/d², same axis×to swirl, same exp(−drag·dt), same soft speed governor, plus per-body sine wander so the cloud never goes crystalline. The only pieces left out: per-body mass variation and a pairwise keep-apart force (each pair of neighbours pushes off, R²/d² − 1, so bodies huddle without overlapping).

05 · the edge of the world

Never teleport a position

The three.js example ends with position = mod(position + half, extent) − half — leave the box, reappear on the far side. Cheap, and a whole bug class: the position jumped without the velocity's permission, so anything that differentiates position — a trail, motion blur, your eye tracking a dot — sees an infinite velocity for one frame. Same sim below, same seeds, one well deliberately roaming past the edge to drag the crowd through the boundary. Left: the wrap. Right: walls made of rung 1 springs — a soft push that only ever changes the velocity.

The streaks slashing across the left box are the teleports, drawn honestly by the trails. The site's rule, enforced by an actual test: positions change only via velocities. If motion must be redirected, push on v and let the loop move p — the mantra already knows how.
